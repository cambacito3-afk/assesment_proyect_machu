History · Historia

Machu Picchu — The Lost City

Built around 1450 during the reign of the Inca emperor Pachacuti, Machu Picchu sits at 2,430 metres above sea level in the Eastern Cordillera of southern Peru, about 80 km northwest of Cusco. Its stones were cut with such precision that no mortar was needed — and during earthquakes, they shift and fall back into place, a remarkable feat of engineering on a site built directly over two fault lines.

Construida alrededor de 1450 durante el reinado del emperador inca Pachacuti, Machu Picchu se eleva a 2.430 m sobre el nivel del mar. Sus piedras fueron cortadas con tal precisión que no necesitan mortero — durante los terremotos, "bailan" y vuelven a su lugar.

Abandoned roughly a century later during the Spanish conquest, the citadel remained largely unknown to the outside world until 1911, when historian Hiram Bingham brought it to international attention. Today it is a UNESCO World Heritage Site (1983) and one of the New Seven Wonders of the World (2007), receiving over 1.5 million visitors annually.

Abandonada con la conquista española, permaneció desconocida hasta 1911. Hoy es Patrimonio de la Humanidad por la UNESCO y una de las Nuevas Siete Maravillas del Mundo.

Construction · Construcción

Engineering without mortar

  • Built using the Inca technique of ashlar — blocks cut to fit perfectly.
  • Over 200 structures covering 81,000 hectares of mountain terrain.
Discovery · Descubrimiento

Hiram Bingham, 1911

  • Yale historian led the expedition that mapped the site for the world.
  • Local farmers had never stopped living on surrounding terraces.

  • Daily visitor numbers are capped to protect the site.

Backpacker Guide · Guía Mochilera

Getting there on a budget

The classic route: take the train from Ollantaytambo to Aguas Calientes — Vistadome cars offer the best river-and-jungle views. From Aguas Calientes, a 25-minute bus brings you to the entrance gate. Prefer to save money? Hike the steep path in about 90 minutes for free. Bring layers — annual rainfall reaches 2,010 mm and humidity stays between 80–90%; weather changes fast at altitude.

La ruta clásica: tren desde Ollantaytambo hasta Aguas Calientes, luego bus de 25 minutos a la entrada. O sube caminando en 90 minutos gratis. Lleva ropa de capas — la humedad supera el 80% y el clima cambia rápido.

Train · Tren

Ollantaytambo → Aguas Calientes

  • Vistadome cars give the best river-and-jungle frames.
  • Book the earliest slot to beat mid-morning crowds.
On foot · A pie

Hike the Sun Gate

  • 90-minute climb from Aguas Calientes — free entry via Inca Trail.
  • Start before 5:30 am to catch sunrise over the citadel.
Tips · Consejos

Pack smart

  • Carry coca tea sachets — altitude sickness hits fast at 2,430 m.
  • Get your passport stamped at the entrance desk — it's free.

Peruvian Cuisine · Cocina Peruana

Eat like a local

Peru was crowned the World's Best Culinary Destination in 2023 — a title it has held for 11 of the past 12 years. Its cuisine is the result of centuries of cultural fusion: indigenous Inca ingredients blended with Spanish, Chinese, Japanese, and African influences. For backpackers, the best food is always found in local markets, not tourist restaurants.

Perú fue elegido Mejor Destino Culinario del Mundo en 2023, título que ha obtenido en 11 de los últimos 12 años. La mejor comida siempre está en los mercados locales, no en los restaurantes turísticos.

Must try · Imprescindible

Ceviche

  • Fresh fish marinated in lime with red onion, chili, sweet potato, and corn.
  • Best found in Lima's Mercado de Surquillo — under $5.
Street food · Calle

Lomo Saltado

  • Beef stir-fried with onion, tomato, chili, and soy sauce — served with fries and rice.
  • Born from Chinese immigrant cooking in the 19th century.
Andean comfort · Andes

Ají de Gallina

  • Shredded chicken in a creamy yellow chili and walnut sauce.
  • The definition of Andean comfort food — try it in Cusco after a long hike.
